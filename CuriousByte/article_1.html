Story of the First Website

How a scientific solution at CERN became the foundation of the modern internet (1991)

Today’s internet feels instant, visual, and deeply connected. But in its earliest form, it was nothing more than a research tool built to solve one very specific problem: sharing scientific information across different computers.

The first website was created in 1991 by British computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee at CERN in Switzerland. At the time, CERN had thousands of researchers working on different machines and systems, making information sharing slow and inconsistent.

The problem before the World Wide Web

Before the World Wide Web existed, there was no universal system for linking or organizing digital documents. Scientists had to manually locate files stored on different computers, often using incompatible formats and separate networks.

This created a major barrier: knowledge existed, but it was fragmented and difficult to access efficiently.

The core challenge was not lack of information — it was the inability to connect and access it across systems.

The breakthrough idea

Tim Berners-Lee proposed a simple but powerful idea: information could be linked using hypertext, allowing documents to reference each other through clickable connections.

This introduced three key technologies: HTML (for structure), HTTP (for communication), and URLs (for addressing resources). Together, they formed the foundation of the World Wide Web.

The first website ever created

The first website was hosted on a NeXT computer at CERN. It was extremely simple and text-based. Its purpose was not entertainment, but explanation — it described what the World Wide Web was and how others could create their own web pages.

Unlike modern websites, it had no images, animations, or styling. It functioned more like a technical manual for an entirely new system.

How the web expanded

After its release, the concept quickly spread among universities and research institutions. Early web browsers made it easier to navigate linked documents, and more websites began to appear worldwide.

This early growth marked the transition from a closed research tool into a global communication system.

Why it changed everything

The World Wide Web fundamentally changed how humans interact with information. It removed barriers between systems and made knowledge accessible through a shared network of linked pages.

This innovation led directly to modern search engines, online platforms, digital communication, and virtually every web-based service we use today.

Legacy of the invention

What started as a solution for scientists at CERN became one of the most influential inventions in history. It transformed communication, education, business, and society as a whole.

The World Wide Web is still built on the same core idea today: simple documents connected through links.
